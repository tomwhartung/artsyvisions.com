The images above are spiritual portraits, or Grojas[1] — images representing the personalities of Patrick Henry, Charles Foster Kane, Clay Davis, and Donald Trump.

The Superficialities

Patrick Henry was one of America's Founding Fathers, the First and Sixth Governor of Virginia, and a close friend of George Washington. Charles Foster Kane is the fictional protagonist of Orson Welles’ film Citizen Kane, 1941. Clay Davis is a fictional politician in David Simon’s television series The Wire, 2002-2008. And Donald Trump is the 45th President of the United States.

All four are American and involved in politics to some degree. Clay Davis is African-American, and Patrick Henry and Donald Trump are red-heads — but Henry frequently wore a brown wig. Charles Foster Kane was created and played by Orson Welles.

Henry and Trump are real people, Kane and Clay exist only in works of fiction — but their characters are based on real people.[2] Henry lived in the eighteenth century, Kane was the product of the twentieth century, and Clay and Trump are contemporary.

Despite these superficial differences, the spiritual portraits of all four look fairly similar. Anyone familiar with one or more of them has a feel for what the others are like.

Passionate Candidates

These spiritual portraits show Henry, Kane, Davis, and Trump are outgoing, passionate, decisive, and realistic. They understand people and do not hesitate to tell them what they believe the people want to hear.

The Red, representing their passion, is the most prominent of these qualities. The Yellow, representing their realism, is less prominent but adds to the portraits' warmth. The Green in Henry's and Trump's portraits show these real people are a bit more decisive and logical than the fictional Kane and Clay.

These outgoing people have so much passion, they enjoy sharing it with — or inflicting it on, as some would say — their constituents. Their willingness to be blunt contributes to their charisma.

Passionate Constituents

As outgoing and decisive people, Henry, Kane, Clay, and Trump are far from being shy, retiring, and contemplative. They relish being the center of attention, their speeches are full of passion, and their temperaments can be volatile.

Making emotional appeals, their proposals for reform are met with mixed sentiments. It is only natural for them to elicit emotional reactions from their supporters and detractors alike.

The passion people feel for these politicians is aptly summed up in the proclamation of the newsreel announcer who introduces Charles Foster Kane to viewers early on in Citizen Kane: Spoke for millions of Americans. Was hated by as many more.

Pragmatic Politicians

The Yellow in these portraits indicate Henry, Kane, Davis, and Trump have a tendency to be realistic and pragmatic.

Realistic About Future Leaders

Patrick Henry's opposition to ratifying the Constitution and insistence on a Bill of Rights is a good example of his pragmatism.

Patrick was concerned the work of Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and the rest of the Constitutional Convention would negate everything they had fought for during the Revolution.

If your American chief be a man of ambition and abilities, Henry warned, how easy is it for him to render himself absolute! The army is in his hands .... If he ever violates the laws ... his crimes [will] teach him to make one bold push for the American throne .... What will then become of you and your rights? Will not absolute despotism ensue?[3]

The Virginia convention eventually voted to ratify the new Constitution, despite Henry's protestations. However, his concerns paved the way for the passage of the Bill of Rights the following year, in 1789.

Realistic About Principles

Charles Foster Kane's pragmatism becomes evident when he writes a Declaration of Principles. The name may be patterned after Thomas Jefferson's Declaration of Independence, but it is not nearly as high-minded as it sounds.

It turns out there are only two statements in Kane’s Declaration, and both start with the personal pronoun I. As Kane’s oldest friend Jedidiah Leland points out, Kane never believed in anything except Charles Kane.

Wealthy Candidates

Another common thread running through the lives of Patrick Henry, Charles Foster Kane, Clay Davis, and Donald Trump is wealth.

Patrick Henry grew up in one of Hanover County's finest houses.[4] Charles Foster Kane and Donald Trump inherited massive amounts of money. And Clay Davis' character first appears in The Wire when his driver picks up a bagful of drug money from the Barksdale organanization.

Whether their wealth is ultimately due to their charismatic appeal, or they feel free to fully express themselves because they are wealthy, is hard to say. Certainly inheriting wealth can help immunize someone to the ill effects of their mistakes.

That is, unlike most people, people born into wealth can do no wrong — and are free do do what ever they feel like doing.

Other Presidents

These images show the personalities of Patrick Henry, Charles Foster Kane, Clay Davis, and Donald Trump are different from the other, more typical leaders.

Out of all of these images, only Patrick Henry's and Donald Trump's show they are ruled by their emotions. The presence of more Red than Green in the spiritual portraits of Henry and Trump represents their tendency to favor emotion over logic.

Lawyers Are Logical

Many politicians start out as lawyers. Governments make and enforce laws.

Understanding the law requires understanding logic. It's if someone commits the crime, then they are guilty, not so-and-so is guilty because I feel like  they are guilty.

Logic is universal, so people are more likely to understand and agree with a logical decision than an emotional one.

Emotions Are Powerful

Once a lawyer understands the logic behind the law, a versatile person could add some emotional appeal to an argument when, for example, adressing a jury.

A lawyer's emotional appeal needs to be secondary and in addition to understanding the logic of the law. If he prefers being emotional to being logical, he needs to know how to let the logic take precedence in his professional work.

This is precisely what Patrick Henry did when he was starting out as a lawyer.

Then, as Henry got older and more experienced, he went from powerfully advocating for his clients to powerfully advocating for individual liberty — ultimately famously declaring Give me liberty, or give me death, in a thunderous speech in Richmond, Virginia.

Atypical Officeholders

Although logical people have an advantage over more emotional people when it comes to running for office, Patrick Henry, Clay Davis, and Donald Trump prove that emotional people can get elected anyway.

Give the People What They Want

Kane’s success in the newspaper business — in addition to his ego — leads him to run for Governor of New York.

Similarly, Donald Trump also found fame in the media, first with his reality TV show The Apprentice, and later with his persistent exhortations that President Obama’s birth certificate is invalid. His opinionated, decisive personality and presence in the media — in addition to his ego — led him to run for President of the United States.

Donald Trump’s election shows he does indeed, like Charles Foster Kane, speak for millions of Americans. And the aftermath of his victory makes it equally plain he is “hated by as many more.”

Both Charles Foster Kane and Donald Trump benefited from large inheritances, enabling them to run their own companies at an early age.

The freedom granted by this wealth means Kane and Trump could hire people at will, tell them to do whatever they felt like telling them to do, and fire them for any offense, big or small, real or imagined. This ability to be tyrannical, along with their opinionated and outspoken manner, fuels the love-hate relationship they have with the public.

Love and hate are two of the strongest human emotions. The spiritual portraits of Charles Foster Kane and Donald Trump both feature a long Vertical Red line on the left side of their images, indicating they are comfortable expressing their emotions.

Differences in Context, Not Substance

The main difference between Charles Foster Kane and Donald Trump — other than the obvious fact that Kane is a fictional character while Trump is real — is not in their personalities, but in the times in which they lived.

Ultimately, this comparison shows a decline in the importance of morality to Americans during the time between Kane’s rise, from the 1900s to 1940s, and Trump’s rise, from the 1970s to the 2010s. Those intervening decades have seen the rise of American exceptionalism after World War II, the invention of the birth control pill and the resultant loosening of sexual morality, and the rise of the internet and social networking — to mention only a very few but major advances.

As abstract images of their personalities, these spiritual portraits of course do not make any attempt to depict these differences, because they are differences in context rather than of substance.

Trying to identify Trump’s ideals and principles leads to a similar conclusion: he doesn’t have any. Does he even have a friend — like Kane’s Jedidiah — brave enough to state he “never believed in anything except” Donald Trump? Given the churn in his administration, it seems unlikely. Trump is so tyrannical and narcissistic that the most cynical of his opponents — and there are many — believe he thinks he’s God.

When Style Reveals Substance

Decades after its production, movie critics continue to admire — and film students continue to learn to appreciate — Citizen Kane more for its revolutionary style than for its substance. One of the most important ways Citizen Kane differs from the films before that time is its narrative structure.

The film actually begins with the death of the protagonist and reveals Charles Foster Kane’s life through a series of flashbacks. The first word uttered in the film — Rosebud — comes just as Kane dies. This ability to see Kane’s entire life as a reporter tries to learn the significance of the word gives viewers an insight into Kane that is currently unattainable with Trump.

Over the course of the film, viewers learn Kane has died a lonely old man. Having reached a pinnacle of success unimaginable to most, in the end he has driven everyone else away with his self-centered, irrational, tyrannical ways. As Kane’s oldest friend, Jedidiah Leland observes, That must have been pretty unpleasant.

It is the flashbacks of Jedidiah Leland — who confesses he may have been more of a stooge than a friend — that give viewers the deepest insight into Kane’s character. In one of the film’s most enlightening scenes Jedidiah claims Charles Foster Kane did everything, from getting married to going into politics, for love.

While most politicians prefer to appeal to logic that everyone can understand — if you commit murder, then you will go to jail — these two prefer to appeal to people’s emotions. Trump made the power of this sort of appeal plain when he infamously claimed he could “shoot somebody” and not “lose any voters.”

During his campaign, Citizen Kane declared he would clean up the current corrupt administration run by Jim W. Gettys. Similarly, during his campaign, Donald Trump declared he would clean up the current, corrupt administration by “draining the swamp.”

The corrupt Gettys ultimately defeats the populist Kane, however, by exposing his extramarital affair with Susan Alexander. It is only natural that, upon losing the election, the headline on the front page of Kane’s Inquirer decried “Fraud at Polls!”

Donald Trump showed he was of a similar mind when during one of the debates he was asked whether he would accept the results if he lost the election. He declared the only way he could lose if if there was fraud at the polls. And even though the he won the electoral vote and hence the election, Trump still insisted he did not win the popular vote because of election fraud.

Can’t Buy Me Love

Most viewers probably get the impression Rosebud is an object, but I assert it is an emotion — represented by the Red line in Kane’s image — his need for love. As Jedidiah observes after Kane is gone: All he really wanted out of life was love. That's Charlie's story, how he lost it. You see, he just didn't have any to give. He loved Charlie Kane, of course. [Chuckles.] Very dearly.

Is this how things will end with Donald Trump? Will he wind up dying a lonely old man, yearning for a love he experienced only as a child, before he was corrupted by wealth he did not earn? Given his volatile temperament, it’s easy to imagine him experiencing a similarly miserable fate.

What will be Donald Trump’s Rosebud? Only time will tell.

Will there be a “film at 11?” Of course there will be — some day! And when it comes to people who are more emotional than logical, that is one of the few things we can predict with certainty.
