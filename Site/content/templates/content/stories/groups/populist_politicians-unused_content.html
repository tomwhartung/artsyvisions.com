Give the People What They Want

Kane’s success in the newspaper business — in addition to his ego — leads him to run for Governor of New York.

Similarly, Donald Trump also found fame in the media, first with his reality TV show The Apprentice, and later with his persistent exhortations that President Obama’s birth certificate is invalid. His opinionated, decisive personality and presence in the media — in addition to his ego — led him to run for President of the United States.

Donald Trump’s election shows he does indeed, like Charles Foster Kane, speak for millions of Americans. And the aftermath of his victory makes it equally plain he is “hated by as many more.”

Both Charles Foster Kane and Donald Trump benefited from large inheritances, enabling them to run their own companies at an early age.

The freedom granted by this wealth means Kane and Trump could hire people at will, tell them to do whatever they felt like telling them to do, and fire them for any offense, big or small, real or imagined. This ability to be tyrannical, along with their opinionated and outspoken manner, fuels the love-hate relationship they have with the public.

Love and hate are two of the strongest human emotions. The spiritual portraits of Charles Foster Kane and Donald Trump both feature a long Vertical Red line on the left side of their images, indicating they are comfortable expressing their emotions.

Differences in Context, Not Substance

The main difference between Charles Foster Kane and Donald Trump — other than the obvious fact that Kane is a fictional character while Trump is real — is not in their personalities, but in the times in which they lived.

Ultimately, this comparison shows a decline in the importance of morality to Americans during the time between Kane’s rise, from the 1900s to 1940s, and Trump’s rise, from the 1970s to the 2010s. Those intervening decades have seen the rise of American exceptionalism after World War II, the invention of the birth control pill and the resultant loosening of sexual morality, and the rise of the internet and social networking — to mention only a very few but major advances.

As abstract images of their personalities, these spiritual portraits of course do not make any attempt to depict these differences, because they are differences in context rather than of substance.

Trying to identify Trump’s ideals and principles leads to a similar conclusion: he doesn’t have any. Does he even have a friend — like Kane’s Jedidiah — brave enough to state he “never believed in anything except” Donald Trump? Given the churn in his administration, it seems unlikely. Trump is so tyrannical and narcissistic that the most cynical of his opponents — and there are many — believe he thinks he’s God.

When Style Reveals Substance

Decades after its production, movie critics continue to admire — and film students continue to learn to appreciate — Citizen Kane more for its revolutionary style than for its substance. One of the most important ways Citizen Kane differs from the films before that time is its narrative structure.

The film actually begins with the death of the protagonist and reveals Charles Foster Kane’s life through a series of flashbacks. The first word uttered in the film — Rosebud — comes just as Kane dies. This ability to see Kane’s entire life as a reporter tries to learn the significance of the word gives viewers an insight into Kane that is currently unattainable with Trump.

Over the course of the film, viewers learn Kane has died a lonely old man. Having reached a pinnacle of success unimaginable to most, in the end he has driven everyone else away with his self-centered, irrational, tyrannical ways. As Kane’s oldest friend, Jedidiah Leland observes, That must have been pretty unpleasant.

It is the flashbacks of Jedidiah Leland — who confesses he may have been more of a stooge than a friend — that give viewers the deepest insight into Kane’s character. In one of the film’s most enlightening scenes Jedidiah claims Charles Foster Kane did everything, from getting married to going into politics, for love.

While most politicians prefer to appeal to logic that everyone can understand — if you commit murder, then you will go to jail — these two prefer to appeal to people’s emotions. Trump made the power of this sort of appeal plain when he infamously claimed he could “shoot somebody” and not “lose any voters.”

During his campaign, Citizen Kane declared he would clean up the current corrupt administration run by Jim W. Gettys. Similarly, during his campaign, Donald Trump declared he would clean up the current, corrupt administration by “draining the swamp.”

The corrupt Gettys ultimately defeats the populist Kane, however, by exposing his extramarital affair with Susan Alexander. It is only natural that, upon losing the election, the headline on the front page of Kane’s Inquirer decried “Fraud at Polls!”

Donald Trump showed he was of a similar mind when during one of the debates he was asked whether he would accept the results if he lost the election. He declared the only way he could lose if if there was fraud at the polls. And even though the he won the electoral vote and hence the election, Trump still insisted he did not win the popular vote because of election fraud.

Can’t Buy Me Love

Most viewers probably get the impression Rosebud is an object, but I assert it is an emotion — represented by the Red line in Kane’s image — his need for love. As Jedidiah observes after Kane is gone: All he really wanted out of life was love. That's Charlie's story, how he lost it. You see, he just didn't have any to give. He loved Charlie Kane, of course. Is this how things will end with Donald Trump? Will he wind up dying a lonely old man, yearning for a love he experienced only as a child, before he was corrupted by wealth he did not earn? Given his volatile temperament, it’s easy to imagine him experiencing a similarly miserable fate.

What will be Donald Trump’s Rosebud? Only time will tell.
