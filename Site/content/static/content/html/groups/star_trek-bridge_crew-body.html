The images above are spiritual portraits, or Grojas.[1] They depict the personalities of crew members on the bridge of the Starship Enterprise on the Original Star Trek series.

Lieutenant Uhura is the starship's communications officer, Lieutenant Sulu is the ship's helmsman, and Ensign Chekov is the navigator. The final image is of their boss, Captain James T. Kirk.

A Diverse Crew?

The crew on the bridge of the Starship Enterprise is interesting because it is racially diverse during a time when American TV was for the most part all-white.

Uhura is African, Sulu is Asian, and Chekov is Russian. Although Chekov is White, he was a Russian and thus part of one of America's most hated enemies in the 1960s. At the time America and Russia were engaged in a Cold War and the paranoid McCarthyism of the 1950s was still fresh in the public's mind.

On the surface, and ignoring the lack of very old or very young crew members for the time being, this crew is quite diverse — especially when viewed in light of the standards for American television during the 1960s.

But that's just on the surface.

A Normal Crew

The crew on the bridge of the Enterprise do not have very diverse personalities, but that's ok. Their personalities are very well-suited for their positions.

The Green and Red dominating Captain Kirk's image represents how he's the decisive one — the boss.

The images of Sulu's and Chekov's personalities show they are very similar. The Yellow in their spiritual portraits shows they are open to new experiences, and the Green shows they prefer working with things rather than people.

It makes sense for Sulu and Chekov to have similar temperaments because, as helmsman and navigator, their jobs are very similar. Indeed, they work side-by side.

Uhuru's image shows she is more emotional than Sulu or Chekov, but otherwise similar in temperament. The Yellow in her spiritual portrait shows she is open to new experiences, and the Red shows she prefers working with people rather than things.

Uhuru's portrait shows she is more emotional and the others more logical, because her position calls for her to work more with people, while Sulu and Chekov work more with the ship.

The contrast between Uhura's and her crew mates' images is far less pronounced than the contrast between the images of Mr. Spock and Dr. McCoy. The images of all three bridge crew members are colorful, showing their preferences are moderate and they are all flexible.

What's Missing?

None of these spiritual portraits has Blue as its dominating color.

The opposite of Yellow — which appears in significant quantities in all of these images — Blue represents a person's tendency to see the world in terms of ideas rather than facts.

There are no visionaries in the bridge crew of the Enterprise. It's not really a good place for visionaries.

Thought: siloed team, frequently taken advantage of by aliens who have the power to exert their will over reality - mind over matter.

In the fictional universe of Star Trek, the visionaries are the people who created the starship, developed the technology it uses, and sent it on its mission. The crew on the bridge are not the ones with the vision, but the ones fulfilling it. They have some Blue, showing they share the vision of those who sent them out, but they are of necessity focused on the realities of day-to-day life.

In the real world, where Star Trek is a TV show struggling for ratings, the visionaries are the writers and producers.
