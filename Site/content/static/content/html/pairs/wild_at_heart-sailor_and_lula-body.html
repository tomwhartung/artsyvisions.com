The images above are spiritual portraits, or Grojas.[1] They depict the personalities of Sailor Ripley and Lula Fortune, the main characters in the film Wild at Heart.

David Lynch wrote the screenplay and directed the film, basing it on the novel by Barry Gifford.

Opening With a Bang

Released on August 17th, 1991, Wild at Heart opens with a violent bang that sets the tone for the rest of the film.

Admittedly a bit brutal in his defense of himself, Sailor Ripley is nonetheless more of a victim than a perpetrator. He quickly pays his debt to society and the film starts in earnest when Lula picks him up at the Pee Dee Correctional Facility. Without further ado, the couple puts all the ugliness behind them and rides off in Lula's black Thunderbird convertible to see their favorite band, the speed-metal group Powermad, at the Hurricane.

Their happiness at being reunited is palpable, and they seem ready to finally enjoy their life together.

A Sweet Couple

The vast amount of Yellow and Blue in their spiritual portraits shows Sailor and Lula are a very sweet couple. They bear no one any ill will and are open to just about everyone and anything.

Lula and Sailor truly enjoy life in each others' company, and they spend their time chatting about the past rather than planning for the future. When it comes time to be decisive, their strong feelings dominate the decisions they make.

Their innocence and passion make it easy for anyone who has ever been young and in love to relate to them. Unfortunately, if Marietta Fortune was ever young and in love, it must have been so long ago that she has completely forgotten that blissful feeling.

A Determined Mom

What Sailor and Lula lack in decision-making ability is more than made up for by Lula's mom Marietta.

For reasons revealed as the film progresses, Marietta has become obsessed with seeing Lula obey her decision to stay away from Sailor. As a teenager who is hopelessly and completely in love, Lula demonstrates time and again she has absolutely no intention of cooperating with her domineering mom, regardless of her mom's feelings.

It would be difficult to create Marietta Fortune's spiritual portrait. because her character is not sufficiently developed in the film. However, it's clear that passion dominates her personality as well.

If there was a way to get to know Marietta better, an image of her personality would certainly be full of Red. She rarely demonstrates any openness towards anyone, except superficially, when it comes time to manipulate a hapless accomplice. Certainly the way Lynch uses Red as a motif throughout the film — particularly in scenes relating to Marietta — is more synchronicity than coincidence.

Blinded by her prejudice, Marietta cannot see that Lula and Sailor just want to be left alone.

Amour Fou — In Hell

The relationship between Sailor and Lula is an excellent example of amour fou,  French for insane love. Precedents for a couple in love fleeing authoritarian figures appear in the 1950 film noir Guncrazy, and the famous gangster film, 1967's Bonnie and Clyde. Despite the superficial similarity, though, the amour fou in Wild at Heart is far from being derivative of those earlier masterpieces.

For one thing, Lula and Sailor are comparatively innocent: Sailor has fully paid the price for being a manslaughterer, and Lula is guilty only of being passionately in love with a man whom her mom refuses to accept. For another, in their attempt to escape Marietta's intolerance, Lula and Sailor find themselves in Hell.

The Inside Scoop

For anyone looking for more about Wild at Heart than the film itself offers, there's Weirdsville USA: The Obsessive Universe of David Lynch, by Paul A. Woods. Published in 2000, the book has a chapter covering each of Lynch's most popular early films, starting with Eraserhead in 1977 and ending with 1999's Mulholland Drive. The chapter Wild at Heart: Lynch Mob Rules appears sandwiched between the two chapters — one for each season — in the book for Twin Peaks.

In Weirdsville USA, Paul A. Woods reveals what the director had to say about the film's big theme:

For those who were looking for the big theme, Wild at Heart was, Lynch assured them, ... about finding love in Hell.
 — David Lynch, quoted in Weirdsville USA by Paul A. Woods, 2000, p.128.

This one bit of insight helps explain some of the seemingly unrelated and otherwise anomalous scenes in the film, such as an apparently random automobile accident and Lula's frustration at not being able to find a suitable radio station.

For fans of David Lynch, the book is great in that it contains lots of inside information like this. Another interesting tidbit from the book that fans will appreciate is, Laura Dern — who plays Lula, is the daughter in real life of Diane Ladd — who plays Lula's mom Marietta.
